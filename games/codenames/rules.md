# Codenames — Complete Rules

**Designer:** Vlaada Chvátil
**Publisher:** Czech Games Edition (CGE)
**Players:** 4–8+ (two teams)
**Play Time:** ~15 minutes
**Year:** 2015

---

## Overview

Codenames is a team word-guessing game in which two rival teams of spies race to identify all of their secret agents on a shared grid of codename cards. Each team has one **Spymaster** who knows which words belong to which team. The Spymaster gives carefully crafted one-word clues to guide their teammates (the **Field Operatives**) toward their team's words — while steering them away from the enemy's agents, innocent bystanders, and the deadly Assassin.

---

## Components

- 200 double-sided codename cards (400 unique words total)
- 16 red agent cards
- 16 blue agent cards
- 1 double agent card
- 7 innocent bystander cards
- 1 assassin card
- 40 key cards (the secret maps)
- 1 card stand (for displaying the key card between spymasters)
- 1 rulebook

---

## Objective

Be the first team to identify all of your team's secret agents on the board. If your team touches the Assassin card at any point, you lose immediately.

---

## Setup

1. **Form two teams.** Teams do not need to be equal in size, but try to make them reasonably balanced in skill. Each team needs at least one player (though the game works best with 2+ per side).

2. **Appoint a Spymaster for each team.** The two Spymasters sit together on the same side of the table, facing their respective Field Operatives across the table.

3. **Lay out the grid.** Shuffle all 200 codename cards and randomly place 25 of them face-up in a 5×5 grid. All players can see all 25 words.

4. **Choose a key card.** One Spymaster randomly draws a key card and places it in the card stand so only the two Spymasters can see it. The key card is a 5×5 map that corresponds to the 25-word grid on the table, showing each word's secret identity:
   - **Blue squares:** Blue team's agents
   - **Red squares:** Red team's agents
   - **Beige/white squares:** Innocent bystanders (civilians)
   - **Black square:** The Assassin

5. **Determine who goes first.** The key card has a colored border (blue or red). That color indicates which team takes the first turn. The starting team has **9 words** to identify; the other team has **8 words**. This asymmetry is the cost of going first.

6. **Give the Double Agent card to the starting team.** The starting team receives the double agent card. This card serves as the 9th agent for the starting team. (It is a single card that counts for one team only — it is not shared.)

7. **Sort and place the tiles.** Stack the blue agent cards in front of the Blue Spymaster, red agent cards in front of the Red Spymaster, and place the bystander and assassin cards where both Spymasters can reach them.

---

## Turn Structure

Teams alternate turns. On each turn:

1. The **Spymaster** gives a clue (one word + one number).
2. The **Field Operatives** discuss among themselves and make guesses, one at a time.
3. The turn ends when the team exhausts their guesses, makes a wrong guess, or voluntarily stops guessing.
4. Play passes to the other team.

---

## The Spymaster's Clue

The Spymaster gives **exactly one word and one number** per turn — nothing more.

### The Word

The clue word is a single word that the Spymaster believes relates thematically to one or more of their team's codenames on the grid.

**Valid clues:**
- Any single English word that connects to the *meaning* of target codenames.
- Proper nouns are allowed if treated as a single word (e.g., "London" or "Tesla").
- Letters or numbers used as clues (e.g., "X" or "7") are allowed if they relate to the word's meaning rather than spelling or grid position.
- Compound words that do not appear on the board are allowed (e.g., "football" is fine if neither "foot" nor "ball" is on the board as a standalone word).

**Invalid clues:**
- Any word currently visible in the 25-card grid.
- Any word that contains, or is contained within, a codename currently on the grid (e.g., if "starfish" is on the board, "star" is not a valid clue).
- Words that sound identical to or are spelled the same as a grid word.
- References to a word's spelling, letter composition, or position on the grid (e.g., "starts with B" or "third row").
- Nonverbal or contextual hints — Spymasters must remain poker-faced and cannot use tone of voice, eye contact, gestures, or pauses to communicate extra information.
- Multiple words (hyphenated words are generally treated as one word; check with your group for compound word edge cases).

### The Number

The number tells Field Operatives how many codenames on the board are related to the clue. This number also determines the maximum number of guesses the team can make.

**Special number values:**

- **Zero (0):** The Spymaster signals that the clue word is *not* connected to any of their team's words — it is a warning to avoid certain cards. With a "0" clue, the operatives may guess as many times as they like (unlimited guesses), but must still make at least one guess.
- **Unlimited (∞):** The clue is genuinely related to one or more words, but the Spymaster declines to specify how many. Operatives may guess as many times as they like until they guess wrong. The risk is that operatives have no idea how many words to go after.

**Standard maximum guesses:** The stated number **plus one**. The "+1" bonus allows teams to catch up on a word they might have missed from a previous clue.

## The Field Operatives' Guesses

After the Spymaster gives a clue, the Field Operatives discuss openly (the Spymaster must stay silent and expressionless) and then make guesses.

### Guessing Procedure

1. Operatives discuss which codename(s) match the clue.
2. One operative physically touches (or clearly indicates) a card to make a guess. Once a card is indicated, the choice is **locked in** — it cannot be changed.
3. The opposing Spymaster (or the active Spymaster, by convention) immediately covers that card with the appropriate tile to reveal its identity.

### Guess Outcomes

| Card Touched | Result |
|---|---|
| **Own team's agent** | Cover with your team's agent card. Team may continue guessing (if guesses remain). |
| **Opposing team's agent** | Cover with the opponent's agent card. That team benefits — and your turn ends immediately. |
| **Innocent bystander** | Cover with a bystander card. Your turn ends immediately. |
| **Assassin** | Cover with the assassin card. **Your team loses the game immediately.** |

### Guess Limits

- **Minimum:** Operatives must make **at least one guess** per turn (they cannot simply pass without guessing).
- **Maximum:** The number given by the Spymaster **plus one** (unless the clue was "0" or "∞," in which case there is no upper limit).
- **Voluntary stop:** At any point after the first guess, operatives may choose to stop guessing and end their turn early (a useful strategic option to avoid risky guesses).

### Catching Up on Old Clues

The "+1" bonus guess per turn exists so operatives can act on a word they failed to guess from a previous turn's clue, even if they fully use their current clue's guesses. However, the bonus is limited to one extra guess — operatives cannot stockpile multiple bonus guesses.

---

## Win and Lose Conditions

### Winning

A team wins immediately when the last of their color's agent cards has been placed on the grid — meaning all of their agents have been identified. This can happen:
- On your own team's turn.
- On the *opposing team's turn* — if the opposing team accidentally guesses one of your words and it happens to be your last remaining agent, **your team wins** (even though it was the other team's turn).

### Losing

A team **loses immediately** if their Field Operatives touch the Assassin card at any point during the game.

### Invalid Clue Penalty (House Rule / Official Clarification)

If a Spymaster gives an invalid clue, the other team may call it out. The invalid turn ends immediately, and the opposing team gets to cover one of their own agent cards as a bonus (effectively advancing themselves one word). This rule encourages Spymasters to be careful and honest.

---

## Spymaster Conduct

- Spymasters may only speak during their turn to deliver the clue (one word + one number).
- Spymasters must remain neutral and expressionless at all times — no sighing, smiling, wincing, or reacting to guesses.
- Spymasters should not confirm, deny, or hint at whether a guess is correct beyond what the tile reveal shows.
- Once the Spymaster has given the clue, they must wait silently while their operatives deliberate.

---

## Field Operative Conduct

- Operatives may talk freely among themselves during their guessing phase.
- Operatives must not look at the key card.
- Operatives may use information from previous turns (e.g., "remember that clue from two turns ago — I think this word still fits").
- Operatives cannot force a teammate to guess a specific card; the team decides collectively.

---

## Special Situations and Edge Cases

### Compound Words
If a codename on the board is part of a compound word (e.g., "fish" on the board), the Spymaster cannot use any compound word that includes it (e.g., "starfish," "swordfish," "fishbowl"). The reverse is also true — if "starfish" is on the board, "star" is not a valid clue. When in doubt, be conservative.

### Homonyms and Multiple Meanings
Clue words may have multiple meanings. A Spymaster may intend one meaning, but operatives are free to interpret any valid meaning of the word. This is part of the game's design — cleverly chosen clues can connect words in unexpected ways.

### Proper Nouns
Proper nouns (names of people, places, brands) are allowed as clues, provided they are a single word and do not appear on the board.

### Letters and Numbers as Clues
Single letters (e.g., "A," "X") and numbers (e.g., "7," "13") are valid clues if they relate to meaning (e.g., "X" for words associated with the letter X as a symbol). They are not valid if they reference the spelling of a codename (e.g., using "A" to mean "words starting with A").

### One or Two Players (Unofficial Variant)
The game is designed for four or more players, but can be adapted for two or three players. In such cases, one player handles both spymaster roles or teams take turns against a set challenge. (Official dedicated two-player rules are found in *Codenames Duet*, a separate product.)

## Strategy Tips

**For Spymasters:**
- Plan several turns ahead — try to link clusters of words under one strong clue.
- Be cautious of any clue that might also connect to the opponent's words, bystanders, or especially the Assassin.
- Using "2" or "3" reliably is often safer than gambling on a large-number clue.
- Consider giving a "1" clue for a word that is dangerously close to the Assassin, to signal your operatives to stay away.

**For Field Operatives:**
- Trust your Spymaster's count — if the clue is "animal, 3," try to identify all three before guessing.
- If you are unsure, take the safe guess (the one least likely to be the Assassin or opponent's word) and stop there.
- Keep track of previous clues — the "+1" bonus guess each turn is your chance to act on a word you hesitated on before.
- Discuss openly, but remember the Spymaster must stay silent — do not try to read their reactions.

## Quick Reference Summary

| Situation | Action |
|---|---|
| Correct guess (own agent) | Cover card; may keep guessing |
| Wrong guess (bystander) | Cover card; turn ends |
| Wrong guess (opponent's agent) | Cover card; opponent benefits; turn ends |
| Wrong guess (Assassin) | Cover card; guessing team loses immediately |
| All agents of one color revealed | That team wins immediately |
| Clue number + 1 | Maximum guesses per turn |
| "0" or "∞" clue | Unlimited guesses until a wrong guess |
